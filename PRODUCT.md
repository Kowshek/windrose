# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Primary, from day one: students, journalists, analysts, think-tank and research juniors, NGO staff, and educators — people who need to track Europe under time pressure and can't afford to read everything themselves. These are all live buyers at launch, not audiences sequenced in later. Universities are a secondary institutional channel today (semester pilots for a program or cohort), reached alongside individuals rather than instead of them.

This is the entry wedge, not the ceiling: the intended long-term primary market is large corporate/enterprise clients. Design and voice should already read credible enough to grow into that buyer, not just serve the student/university audience it's launching with.

## Product Purpose

Windrose is a daily Europe intelligence brief: 5–8 triaged items a day, each with what happened, why it matters, and what to watch, backed by a published and scored methodology so the sourcing and confidence behind every call are visible. A forward events/risk calendar shows what's coming — summits, elections, deadlines, flashpoints — not just what already broke.

The product is pre-launch. This site's current job is collecting waitlist signups from individuals and opening pilot conversations with university departments and libraries ahead of launch.

## Positioning

Analyst-grade monitoring of Europe, triaged and sourced by a published, transparent methodology, priced within reach for a student today — built to scale into the tier a corporate or institutional buyer would trust tomorrow.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: Join the waitlist (individuals)
- Secondary CTA: Start a pilot conversation (universities, via email — explicitly framed as a conversation, not a checkout)
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: "See what's coming. Not just what broke."
- Belief ladder: Europe news is overwhelming and reactive → Windrose triages it into a few daily items with the method shown, not just more headlines → it looks forward (risk/events calendar), not only backward → the method is transparent and scored, so it can be checked, not just trusted on faith → it's priced within reach right now, worth joining before launch.
- Proof on hand: none yet — pre-launch, no testimonials, press, or partner logos collected. The daily-brief preview and the stated methodology are the proof surface until real usage exists.

## Brand Personality

Rigorous, discreet, forward-looking. The register of a serious analyst briefing, not a breaking-news alert: confident without hype, urgent without alarm. No named references or anti-references were given — the current direction (dark, editorial, restrained glass, serif display type) is the trusted baseline; extend it rather than reinvent it.

## Anti-references

None named explicitly. Given the personality above and the long-term enterprise ambition, the implicit failure mode to avoid is drifting toward either extreme: loud breaking-news urgency (tickers, alert-red, panic pacing) on one side, or a visibly "cheap student tool" look on the other. Both undercut the credibility the brand needs to keep as its buyer shifts upmarket.

## Design Principles

Credibility that scales: the wedge is students and universities, but nothing in the design should read as a ceiling — it has to already look like something a corporate or institutional buyer would trust later. Affordable must never curdle into cheap.

The method is the product: sourcing, confidence, and scoring are shown, not hidden in a footnote. Transparency is the trust mechanism the whole brand leans on, not a compliance afterthought.

Forward over reactive: every surface should weight what's coming over what already happened. Windrose sells foresight, not a faster recap.

Earn the ask in sequence: hook, evidence of value, proof of format, methodology trust, then the CTA. Don't front-load the ask before the case is made.

Quiet confidence over urgency: analyst-briefing tone throughout — measured, not alarmed.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as the baseline. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` everywhere motion is used — several components already gate GSAP/scroll animation behind it; keep that consistent as new sections are added. No further accessibility requirements were specified.
